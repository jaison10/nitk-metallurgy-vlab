Optical microscopy of non ferrous samples

Objective

To obtain microstructures of non ferrous alloys like Solder alloy (Pb-Sn) and Brass.


Apparatus used:

Metallurgical microscope, polishing machine with abrasives, belt grinder, abrasive paper, flat glass plate with wooden framework, electric hot air blower, cotton, nital reagent (etchant), specimen.


Description Non-ferrous alloys : Non-ferrous metals or alloys are materials that are not iron-based like their ferrous counterparts. Non-ferrous metals are found in the Earth as chemical compounds. The most important non-ferrous metals happen to be oxides or sulfides.

Magnification on a microscope : This refers to the amount or degree of visual enlargement of an observed object. Magnification is measured by multiples, such as 50X, 100X and 500X, indicating that the object is enlarged to 50 times as big, 100 times as big or 500 times as big, respectively.
STEP  1 

Select a sample to analyse its microstructure.



Lead based solder is the composition of 60/40 (tin/lead) blend with a melting point around 180-190°C.

Used to solder metal workpieces together.
Brass is an alloy made from copper and zinc and a small percentage of other metals like iron, nickel, lead, tin, aluminum and antimony.

Used for for applications where low friction is required such as locks, gears, bearings, ammunition, and valves.
STEP  2 

Obtain a flat surface on both specimens by belt grinding.


STEP  3 

Support a polish paper of 1/0 fineness on a flat glass plate. Polish the specimens rubbing in forward direction only.



1/0 Polish paper Note:

Move to the next finer paper (2/0 and 3/0) once the scratches on the specimen are uniform. While changing from one paper to another, the specimen and your hands should be cleaned free from the abrasive of the previous paper, and the specimen turned through 90°, so that the scratches from the previous paper are at right angles to the scratches from the next paper.

   
STEP  4 

Finish polishing the specimens on 4/0 abrasive paper, wash hands and specimens.



4/0 Polish paper
STEP  5 

Do the fine polishing on the disc polishing unit.



OFF
Note:
After fine polishing, clean off the polished surface of the specimens with water and dry it with an electric hot air blower.

   
STEP  6 

Etch both specimens with Nital 2% reagent by swabbing for a few seconds. Wash and dry.



Note:

Nital reagent : Nital is the name given to an oxidizing etchant solution composed of aqueous nitric acid and ethanol.

   
STEP  7 

Examine both specimens under the microscope and compare the structural features.

Mild Steel Grey Cast Iron
Number of phases : 2 (Mild Steel)
Features of each phase :
  • Ferrite: The lighter region represents this phase. It forms the soft α-matrix. It consists of pro-eutectoid ferrite mainly.
  • Pearlite: The darker region represents this phase. It has a lamellar morphology which can be observed at higher magnification (at 500x). It imparts strength to the material. It consists of ferrite and cementite (Fe2O3) arranged alternatively.
Number of phases : 3 (Grey Cast Iron)
Features of each phase:
  • Graphite: Flaky graphite
  • Pro-eutectoid Ferrite: Concentrated at the graphite-pearlite grain boundaries, whiter compared to other phases.
  • Pearlite: Matrix, darker than ferrite. A lamellar structure consisting of alternate layers of ferrite and cementite.
